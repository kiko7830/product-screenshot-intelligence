# Product Screenshot Intelligence Framework

## Evidence Ladder

Use the highest available evidence level:

1. Direct screenshot evidence: visible brand, URL, logo, product copy, feature names.
2. Primary product evidence: official website, docs, help center, pricing, changelog, release notes, app store listing.
3. Market evidence: filings, analyst reports, reputable news, review platforms, investor materials.
4. Inference: category patterns, UX conventions, competitor comparison, strategic reasoning.

State which level supports each important claim.

## Product Identification Checklist

- Brand: logo, wordmark, favicon, app name, workspace name.
- URL/domain: browser bar, email domain, deeplink, app store package.
- UI copy: feature labels, empty states, onboarding text, settings names.
- Visual system: typography, component style, icon set, navigation structure.
- Data model: objects such as campaigns, tasks, tickets, pipelines, contacts, models, prompts, wallets.
- Integrations: third-party logos, connected accounts, import/export options.
- Category clues: pricing unit, dashboard metrics, permissions, reports, workflows.

## Market Analysis Checklist

- Category and subcategory.
- Main competitors and substitutes.
- Market maturity: emerging, expanding, consolidating, saturated.
- Differentiation: product capability, workflow depth, data advantage, ecosystem, price, distribution.
- Monetization: seat-based SaaS, usage-based, transaction fee, marketplace, ads, hardware, services.
- Growth channels: PLG, sales-led, community, enterprise procurement, marketplace distribution, integrations.
- Current momentum: recent launches, funding, layoffs, acquisitions, regulatory pressure, platform shifts.

## Customer Segmentation

Distinguish:

- User: person operating the feature.
- Buyer: person approving budget.
- Admin: person configuring governance, permissions, compliance, or integrations.
- Beneficiary: person or team receiving the output.

Common segmentation dimensions:

- Company size: individual, SMB, mid-market, enterprise.
- Role: founder, PM, designer, engineer, sales, marketer, support, finance, ops, HR, legal.
- Sophistication: beginner, power user, technical operator, regulated enterprise.
- Use frequency: daily workflow, periodic reporting, one-off task, crisis/incident use.
- Willingness to pay: time saved, revenue gained, risk reduced, quality improved, compliance achieved.

## Strengths And Weaknesses

Analyze across five surfaces:

- Product: breadth, depth, ease of use, reliability, performance, data quality.
- UX: learnability, workflow fit, speed, error prevention, collaboration, accessibility.
- Business: pricing power, retention, expansion, sales motion, gross margin, procurement friction.
- Distribution: SEO, community, partnerships, marketplace, integrations, brand.
- Defensibility: data network effects, workflow lock-in, ecosystem, trust, switching cost, regulatory approvals.

## Feature Strategic Waterline

Classify the screenshot feature by its dominant role:

- Core workflow: necessary for the product's main job-to-be-done.
- Activation: helps new users reach first value.
- Growth: increases acquisition, virality, sharing, invites, referrals, SEO, or marketplace supply/demand.
- Retention: creates habit, workflow depth, saved state, collaboration, alerts, or reporting loops.
- Monetization: supports paywalls, packaging, usage metering, upsell, enterprise plans.
- Trust and compliance: handles permissions, auditability, privacy, security, governance, reliability.
- Operational efficiency: reduces support, manual work, onboarding load, or internal cost.
- Experimental option: tests demand, positioning, or a future product direction.

## Short-Term vs Long-Term Role

Short-term analysis:

- Which immediate metric might this move? Activation, conversion, task completion, time-to-value, retention, ARPU, support tickets, sales cycle, trust.
- Which urgent customer pain does it remove?
- What adoption behavior should change in the next 30-90 days?
- What implementation risk or UX risk exists now?

Long-term analysis:

- Does it deepen the core workflow or only patch a gap?
- Does it create proprietary data, repeat usage, collaboration, or switching cost?
- Does it expand the product into a broader platform, adjacent persona, or higher willingness-to-pay segment?
- Does it defend against competitors or commoditization?
- Does it create maintenance, privacy, compliance, or strategic distraction risk?

## Why Build / Why Now

Explain using the strongest relevant reasons:

- Customer pull: repeated requests, workflow blockage, enterprise procurement requirement.
- Competitive pressure: table-stakes parity, differentiation, platform shift.
- Business leverage: conversion, expansion, retention, margin, sales enablement.
- Strategic foundation: unlocks future features, data model, ecosystem, compliance, integrations.
- Timing: new technology, regulation, customer maturity, market education, cost curve change.

Also state the counterargument: why the team might defer or avoid building it.

## Report Tone

Be decisive but careful. Use plain language and short strategic judgments. Avoid presenting generic frameworks as if they were product-specific facts.
